MLB Hall of Fame Predictor

Overview

Come election time for the Major League Baseball Hall of Fame, various players, commentators, and writers, attempt to predict who will and will not gain entry. We have chosen to build a system that, given the lifetime statistics of a Major League Baseball position player, predicts whether or not that position player will be inducted into the Hall of Fame. We construct two Gaussian models: one that is representative of Hall of Famers and another that is representative of players not in the Hall of Fame. We studied 15 offensive statistics in particular: G, PA, HR, R, RBI, SB, ISO, BABIP, AVG, OBP, SLG, wOBA, wRC+, BsR, and WAR.

Methodology

We utilized multi-dimensional Gaussians to model our Hall of Fame class and our non-Hall of Fame class, which we will call the "Scrub" class. We trained our system on data from the years 1974-1984 (this is intuitive since you need at least 10 years of retirement to be eligible for the HOF). Once we created our training set, we performed 3-fold cross validation and proceeded to divide up the training sets into Hall-of-Famers and Scrubs, from which we based our Gaussian models. Using both models, if we find that the probability of a player belonging to the HOF class is greater than the probability of him belonging to the Scrub class then he is classified as a Hall-of-Famer, otherwise he is a Scrub. Thus, the error measure for our model is the number of missclassifications on the validation sets. The baseline approach against which we will compare our system is the assumption that the probability of a player making the Hall of Fame is .062 (or the proportion of Hall-of-Famers in our training set). The baseline will then proceed to classify every player as a Scrub and will have an error rate of .062.

Results

Thusfar, we have determined that our system is about as accurate as using the baseline approach if we use advanced metrics and worse if we use typical metrics. For instance, when we create our Gaussian models using HR and RBI, we obtain an average error rate of .137. When using the advanced metrics of wOBA, wRC+, and WAR, we obtain an average error rate of approximately .066, very similar to the baseline error rate of .062.
